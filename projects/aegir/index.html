AEGIR: Modeling Area Emitters for Indoor Inverse Rendering Using Gaussian Splatting

Mohamed Shawky Sabae, Philipp Langsteiner, Jan-Niklas Dihlmann, Hendrik Lensch

Computer Graphics Group, Eberhard Karls University of Tübingen

arXiv preprint, 2026

Paper Code

Abstract

Inverse rendering must separate illumination from surface materials, but indoor images tightly couple the two. Existing relightable Gaussian Splatting methods often use point lights, environment maps, or implicit light fields, which struggle with finite-area emitters and soft local shadows.

AEGIR adds explicit local area emitters to a relightable Gaussian Splatting representation. It jointly optimizes emitters, geometry, and PBR materials with a differentiable deferred renderer, improving lighting reconstruction and enabling controlled scene edits.

Method

AEGIR represents indoor light sources as localized anisotropic 3D primitives with learnable shape, color, orientation, scale, and angular emission. Optimizing these emitters with 2D Gaussian geometry and PBR materials lets lighting explain shadows and highlights instead of baking them into albedo.

Area Emitters

Each emitter uses anisotropic scale and a Super-Gaussian angular falloff to model lights ranging from compact bulbs to elongated ceiling fixtures.

Deferred Rendering

A deferred G-buffer stores albedo, roughness, metallic, and normals; illumination uses a microfacet BRDF, emitter samples, hemisphere samples, and visibility tracing.

Joint Optimization

Photometric supervision, diffusion material priors, edge-aware TV, light regularization, and a final emitter-only refinement stabilize the decomposition.

Area Emitter Formulation

AEGIR uses ellipsoidal emitters with independent spatial scales and angular spread. A Super-Gaussian falloff lets one primitive adapt between broad diffuse lights and focused directional sources.

Lighting Estimation

AEGIR evaluates lighting in a controlled Mitsuba pipeline by fixing ground-truth geometry and materials, then re-rendering each scene with recovered lights. Across indoor and environment lighting, AEGIR best matches the reference render and recovers smoother illumination than point-light or environment-map baselines.

Novel View Synthesis and Intrinsic Decomposition

Across ScanNet++, Replica, and FIPT-Real, AEGIR reports the best real-world NVS scores. On synthetic benchmarks, it also improves albedo recovery, showing that explicit emitters reduce illumination leakage into material maps.

Applications

Because lighting is represented as editable geometric emitters, AEGIR supports controlled relighting and more plausible virtual object insertion under local indoor illumination.
